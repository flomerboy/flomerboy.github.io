耳多 / @ll ears

education design, interaction design, UI/UX

Design a fun way for English and Chinese speakers to learn each others' language .

explore mode

The tools are placed in a way that they are ergonomically easier to press depending on how useful they are from a pedagogical standpoint. (i.e. the play button is the easiest to press at the beginning because it is good for students to listen before speaking or studying the grammar). I based this off of the standard model taught at Brown University (where I study Chinese):
  1. listen 5 times
  2. read Aloud 5 times
  3. study Grammar/english meaning

giving feedback

Although people are generally poor teachers of their own language, the best language learning happens when you can exchange with someone who is a native speaker. This is why "exchange" is a very important feature to 耳多／@ll ears.
When someone enters the "exchange" mode, they have the option to either submit a recording or provide feedback. The ergonomics/choice architecture is designed in such a way that users are encouraged to give personalized feedback. That is why the "record audio suggestion" button is closer to the home button than the "offer textual advice" button. When in the textual advise section, the shallow remarks are placed further away from the home position than the others.

receiving feedback

Another very important part of the feedback ecosystem of 耳多／@ll ears is the quality of feedback. I've designed the interface for viewing feedback in a way that it looks exciting and fun to receive feedback. The feedback hangs on the audio like fruit and pops up playfully as the audio goes by. If the feedback-giver records audio, the recording pauses and plays the feedback through before resuming. At the end, the recipient of feedback records whether or not they found the feedback useful. This statistic, combined the with the activity of users, combines to create a "helpful user" score, which sorts good users with new users, and other good users.

Process:

intitial wireframes & process

More process coming soon! In short my process was:
  1. branding concepting
  2. physical prototyping with paper
  3. wireframing
  4. illustrations
  5. digitizing everything
Steps 3-5 saw a lot of churn.
More process coming very soon as I begin to test the functionality of the app with real- live students! Here is my plan for testing:
  1. test efficiency of learning structure
  2. test "legibility" of icons
  3. test the feedback average Chinese speakers give to novice Chinese with an English accent; what is the most common feedback?
  4. test the feedback that average English speakers give to novice English speakers with a Chinese accent; what kind of feedback comes up all the time?
  5. stay open to unexpected things!

first user testing

For this round of user testing, I put the dialogue screen on my computer, and instead of the user pressing buttons - I would just speak out loud and say what the app would say. I noticed very quickly that I needed to explain to people how to read pinyin (although I think I should test this again later) since some of the sounds in pinyin have no english equivalent (j and q are the hardest). Some other insights are that the pinyin should come underneath the characters - not replace them, and that when users record their own audio, the recording should play just before hand (except for advanced learners). There was a huge variability in learning speed, which I'm guessing is because of varied experience levels and to what extent the participants are "good with languages", this just means that I should make sure I have a high n value in future testing sessions.

For my analysis, I'm calculating accuracy by dividing the number of 'wrong' words (words in which the tone, consonants, or vowel are mispronounced the the point that it is a different word), and dividing it by the # of total words (30), then subtracting that from 1.
